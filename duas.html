Supplications

Du'as of Patience & Steadfastness

Authentic prophetic supplications that reflect the spirit of this era — bearing hardship with trust, holding faith under pressure, and seeking ease from the One who grants it.

A note on dating: Most authentic prophetic du'as are not specifically dated to 610–613 CE in the classical sources — the Seerah does not record a running log of which supplication was said on which day. This page does not claim each du'a was literally said during these exact three years. Rather, these are authentic supplications from verified sources whose themes — patience in hardship, steadfastness of faith, trust in Allah's relief, and comfort for the persecuted — are precisely the spirit this era embodies. The one exception is the narration of the Prophet ﷺ comforting the family of Yasir, which is specifically tied to this period in the sources.
Patience in Hardship

The Du'a for Patience (Sabr)

اللَّهُمَّ إِنِّي أَسْأَلُكَ الصَّبْرَ عِنْدَ الْبَلَاءِ، وَالثَّبَاتَ عِنْدَ اللِّقَاءِ، وَالشُّكْرَ عِنْدَ الرَّخَاءِ

Allāhumma innī as'aluka al-ṣabra 'inda al-balā', wa al-thabāta 'inda al-liqā', wa al-shukra 'inda al-rakhā'

"O Allah, I ask You for patience in times of trial, steadfastness at the moment of encounter, and gratitude in times of ease."

Source Recorded by al-Nasa'i in Amal al-Yawm wa al-Layla and al-Tabarani. Authenticated by al-Albani.
Connection to This Era

The three qualities asked for here — patience under trial, steadfastness in confrontation, gratitude in ease — are exactly what the first Muslim community had to cultivate across the secret years. Bilal, Sumayyah, and Yasir lived this du'a before it was formally recorded.

Steadfastness of Faith

Yā Muqallib al-Qulūb — O Turner of Hearts

يَا مُقَلِّبَ الْقُلُوبِ ثَبِّتْ قَلْبِي عَلَى دِينِكَ

Yā Muqallib al-qulūb, thabbit qalbī 'alā dīnik

"O Turner of hearts, make my heart firm upon Your religion."

Source Jami' al-Tirmidhi, Hadith 3522. Narrated by Anas ibn Malik (RA). Graded Hasan.
Connection to This Era

The Prophet ﷺ recited this supplication frequently, to the point that Umm Salamah (RA) asked him about it. In a community where conversion meant exile from tribe and family, holding firm to faith was a daily, active act — this du'a names that struggle directly.

Ease After Difficulty

The Du'a for Relief from Distress

اللَّهُمَّ لَا سَهْلَ إِلَّا مَا جَعَلْتَهُ سَهْلًا، وَأَنْتَ تَجْعَلُ الْحَزْنَ إِذَا شِئْتَ سَهْلًا

Allāhumma lā sahla illā mā ja'altahu sahlā, wa anta taj'alu al-ḥazna idhā shi'ta sahlā

"O Allah, there is no ease except in what You make easy, and You, if You will, make grief and hardship easy."

Source Ibn Hibban in his Sahih (no. 2427) and Ibn al-Sunni in Amal al-Yawm wa al-Layla. Authenticated by Ibn Hibban and al-Albani.
Connection to This Era

During the three secret years, the believers had no worldly power to make their path easy — only divine facilitation. This du'a captures precisely that reliance: not asking for the hardship to disappear, but for Allah to transform it. The Qur'anic reminder "with every hardship comes ease" (94:5–6) echoes through every line of this era's story.

Protection from Fitnah

Seeking Refuge from the Trial of Faith

اللَّهُمَّ إِنِّي أَعُوذُ بِكَ مِنَ الْفِتَنِ مَا ظَهَرَ مِنْهَا وَمَا بَطَنَ

Allāhumma innī a'ūdhu bika min al-fitan, mā ẓahara minhā wa mā baṭan

"O Allah, I seek refuge in You from trials — those that are apparent and those that are hidden."

Source Sahih Muslim, Book of Remembrance and Supplication (Kitab al-Dhikr), narrated from the du'as the Prophet ﷺ taught for seeking refuge from trials. Confirmed authentic.
Connection to This Era

The fitna of the secret years was both visible (torture of Bilal, the martyrdom of Sumayyah) and hidden (the constant pressure to recant faith quietly, or to slow the growth of the community out of fear). This du'a addresses both dimensions at once.

Specifically Narrated — This Era

The Prophet's ﷺ Words to the Family of Yasir

صَبْرًا آلَ يَاسِرٍ، فَإِنَّ مَوْعِدَكُمُ الْجَنَّةُ

Ṣabran āla Yāsir, fa inna maw'idakumu al-jannah

"Patience, O family of Yasir — your appointed place is Paradise."

Source Mustadrak al-Hakim (no. 5652), narrated from Ibn Abbas (RA) and Ammar ibn Yasir (RA). Also referenced in al-Haythami's Majma' al-Zawa'id. Authenticated by al-Hakim.
Specifically From This Era

Unlike the other du'as on this page, this narration is explicitly tied to 610–613 CE in the sources. The Prophet ﷺ passed by Yasir and Sumayyah as they were being tortured in the midday heat of Makkah — unable to physically intervene due to the tribal honour code — and offered them these words of certainty. Sumayyah died shortly after, becoming Islam's first martyr. Read their full story →

Trust in Allah (Tawakkul)

Hasbunallah wa Ni'ma al-Wakil

حَسْبُنَا اللَّهُ وَنِعْمَ الْوَكِيلُ

Ḥasbunallāhu wa ni'ma al-wakīl

"Allah is sufficient for us, and He is the best Disposer of affairs."

Source Sahih al-Bukhari, Hadith 4563. These words are from the Qur'an (3:173), and are also the words the Prophet Ibrahim (AS) said when cast into the fire — a narration cited in Bukhari connecting the phrase to moments of extreme trust in Allah.
Connection to This Era

This phrase from Surah Al 'Imran was spoken by the early believers when told that a great army had gathered against them — yet it increased their faith. The first Muslim community of Makkah faced a comparable moment every day: outnumbered, unprotected, and dependent entirely on Allah's protection. This is the declaration they lived by.

From Words to Lives

These supplications only make sense when read alongside the people who needed them most. The pioneers and the persecuted of the secret era are their living commentary.
